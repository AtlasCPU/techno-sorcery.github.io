My first homemade radio, born out of boredom and a longing to know how wireless communications work.
Last updated ~ 5/17/2022 (Added antenna tap slider)

Crystal Set, Mk.1

About
This is the first crystal set I've ever built, outside of activities that came with those "200-in-1 Science Fair" kit. At the time of building, I didn't exactly know enough to create my own unique design. However, I figured it would be a perfect introduction to the world of radio communications. Thus, I decided to construct the project in two phases-- first, I'd make an unpowered crystal reciever. Then, I'd tack-on a simple one-transistor amplifier.

The reciever
For the crystal reciever, I used a modified version of rimstar's "scrap radio" design. Because I didn't have a neat-and-tidy pre-built one, I chose to ommit the variable capacitor (for the time being). Although rimstar showed how to make your own variable capacitor out of two towel tubes and some aluminum foil, I found his solution to be somewhat unsigntly.



The hardest part of the entire project was building the tuning coil. Preventing the wire from springing off or losing tension as I wound it was difficult-- I had two false-starts before I managed to nail down the technique. Although I didn't exactly pay careful attention to how many turns I would, I believe the primary (antenna) coil had 25 windings, and the secondary coil had 150-160 windings. As a way of tuning the radio, I sanded off some of the wire's varnish and used a custom sliding mechanism. As I slid the tuner across the coil, a metal nail would make contact with the bare wire, completing the circuit.



The first time I tested the radio, it barely worked. I could pick up some nearby Mexican station, but only very, very faintly. Obviously, this wasn't the result I desired. Thinking the simple point-to-point antenna I strung out in the backyard might not be good enough, I built a nifty little loop antenna. However, this failed to rectify the problem. Then, it came to me: the three-inch metal stake I was using as a grounding rod might not be good enough. I decided to clip the ground wire to a water pipe and... Aha! Problem solved.

A simple amplifier
Although I was getting much louder audio than I had before, the radio was only really able to pick up super close-by signals. At the same time, through inductance, the antenna would be almost completely ineffective if not placed on a large metal object. I felt it would be in order to build an amplifier, something I had planned on doing from the start.



I decided on a relatively simple circuit from techlib.com, which only needed a single transistor. It could also be bolted on right where the earphone would have normally connected, meaning I could very easily add it on to the crystal reciever.



With its new amplifier, the radio was much louder-- even loud enough to distort the audio output on some stations. However, I still had the issue of selectivity, or rather a lack thereof. I couldn't just listen to one station at a time, I had to mentally sift through three or more undesired ones. It was expecially bad with the Mexican station, since the signal was so strong that it would overpower everything else.



In order to better my set's selectivity, I added a sliding "tap", similar to the ones featured on "dual-slider" crystal radios of old. This way, I could directly connect the antenna to any point on the coil, further prioritizing desired frequencies while canceling-out the others. I also did some experiments with adding a variable capacitor between my antenna, and the tap point. As the capacitor was part of a "200-in-1 Science Lab Kit" and I don't feel like canibalizing it, I'm going to order one off ebay.

I also cut the wooden board in half, allowing for easier transport. I'm going to repurpose the other half in the future, probably to build a short-distance AM transmitter.
